01Problem Statement

Young Australians aged 18–29 are cutting back on healthcare in real terms — not because they're healthy, but because the system is opaque. Most don't know what bulk-billing means, what Medicare covers them for, or what care will cost before they show up. When something feels wrong, 37% go to Google first. The information to help them exists. The entitlements are already funded. The problem is that no one has made the system legible for the generation navigating it alone for the first time.

01Rule Set A — The 4 Layers of Safe AI
Non-negotiable: Every feature, every AI output, every dataset integration must pass through all 4 layers before being built, documented, or shipped. If something fails a layer check it must be revised — not worked around.
L1
Data Grounding — No Hallucination
RAG over verified government sources only
All AI outputs must be grounded in verified government source data via RAG
No AI feature may generate information from model memory alone
Every response must be traceable to a specific government document
SentenceTransformers + pgvector inside Azure PostgreSQL — no separate vector DB needed
Vector embeddings must be re-indexed every time source data updates (MBS updates quarterly)
Confidence score must be surfaced to the user for every AI match
L2
Hard Fencing — What AI Cannot Do
Enforced at system prompt AND application layer
AI must never diagnose, assess clinical risk, or recommend specific treatments
System prompt rules are non-negotiable and enforced at application layer — not just trusted to the model
Emergency escalation (Call 000) must be triggered automatically for any high-risk input
The line is always: Navigate, never diagnose
PathwayAI says "may be relevant to your situation" — never "you qualify for"
RightDoor never suppresses the ED option regardless of urgency score
L3
Legal Disclaimers — Built Into UI
Not fine print — visible on every screen
"This is not medical advice" — on every screen, always visible
"A GP makes the final clinical decision" — on every PathwayAI and EntitleCheck result
"Information sourced from [document + date]" — on every entitlement card
"In an emergency, call 000" — permanently visible in app header, one tap away
"Last updated: [date]" — because MBS data changes quarterly
Cost estimates labelled "Confirm with provider before attending" — never "guaranteed free"
L4
Human Override — Always
AI is never the last step — a human always is
Every AI feature must end with a clear path to a real human
Healthdirect 1800 022 222 always accessible from every screen
Call 000 always one tap away — never buried in a menu
PathwayAI: AI surfaces entitlement → Jordan books GP → GP makes clinical call
RightDoor: AI gives options → Jordan decides → human provider delivers care
No feature reaches a terminal state without a human action available
02The 3 Master Rules
01
Navigate, Never Diagnose
MediClear tells Jordan where to go and what to ask. It never tells Jordan what is wrong with them. If any output could be interpreted as a clinical determination — stop and revise.
02
Estimate, Never Guarantee
Every cost, every eligibility match, every wait time is an estimate sourced from open data. A human confirms the final answer. Label every estimate clearly — never present data as certain.
03
Inform, Never Decide
PathwayAI gives Jordan the knowledge to have a better GP conversation. The GP makes every clinical and eligibility decision. The app empowers — it does not replace professional judgement.

05TGA Compliance — The Regulatory Boundary
TGA risk: The Therapeutic Goods Administration regulates software intended to diagnose, prevent, monitor, treat or alleviate a disease or condition. Crossing this line — even accidentally — classifies MediClear as a regulated medical device requiring registration. Every feature must be demonstrably on the navigation side of this line.
✅ Navigation Tool (Not Regulated)
Displaying Medicare entitlements. Showing provider locations and costs. Comparing care pathways by cost and distance. Surfacing questions to ask a GP. Providing preventive health reminders based on public guidelines.
❌ Medical Device (Regulated)
Diagnosing conditions from symptoms. Determining clinical eligibility for Medicare items. Assessing whether a user needs emergency care. Recommending specific treatments or medications. Risk-scoring individual health conditions.
06Privacy Act 1988 — Health Data Obligations
Australian Privacy Act 1988 — Key obligations for MediClear
Health information = sensitive informationAge, sex, health schedule, and entitlement data stored in Azure PostgreSQL is classified as sensitive information under the Act and attracts the highest level of protection. Explicit consent required before collection.
Required at launchPrivacy policy clearly stating: what is collected, why, how it is stored, who can access it, and how to request deletion. Data deletion mechanism must be built into the account settings. Azure PostgreSQL encryption at rest is mandatory.

02Who · What · Where · Why · How
👤WHO is Jordan?
Jordan moved out of home at 19 onto their own Medicare card. They earn an irregular income in hospitality — sometimes full weeks, sometimes nothing — making financial planning including healthcare genuinely hard. They didn't grow up with parents who explained the health system. They've never had a regular GP and wouldn't know how to find an affordable one. They're not disengaged from health as a concept — they care — but the system feels like it was built for someone else.
🤔WHAT does Jordan experience?
Jordan experiences the health system as opaque and anxiety-inducing. They've shown up to a GP and been surprised by a $70 out-of-pocket gap. That happened once — they haven't gone back since. They know they should be getting skin checks (grew up in Queensland), blood tests, and dental visits. But "should do" and "will do" are separated by: How much will it cost? Where do I go? Do I need a referral? Is this worth it? The back pain from standing 8 hours a day is dismissed as "just part of the job."
📍WHERE does Jordan seek help?
First stop: Google. A symptom, a question, a worry — it goes into a search bar. Jordan doesn't distinguish between reliable health information and the first result.

Second stop: texting a friend. "Is this normal?" is a common 11pm message.

Third stop (only if urgent): hospital ED. Been once with a bad infection. Four-hour wait. Determined not to repeat it — but doesn't know where else to go if something feels serious.
💡WHY doesn't Jordan engage?
Cost uncertainty is the primary barrier. It's not that Jordan can't afford $0 — it's that they don't know if it will be $0 or $95 until they're at the counter. That asymmetry is enough to avoid it entirely.

Navigation complexity is the secondary barrier. The Medicare website, MyGov, and Healthdirect feel like they were written for people who already understand the system.

Absence of urgency is the third. Nothing has gone seriously wrong yet. The "someday" list keeps growing indefinitely.
📱HOW does Jordan want to be helped?
Plain language, not jargon"This is free for you" beats "eligible for Medicare bulk-billing rebate under item 715." Every term must be explained in one sentence without requiring a glossary.
Cost shown upfront, alwaysThe number one thing that would change Jordan's behaviour: knowing what something costs before committing. Not a range. Not "it depends." A specific honest estimate.
Mobile-first, no account neededJordan won't create an account for a health app. They'll use it on their phone during a 10-minute break at work. The entire value must be delivered in under 60 seconds.

Key StatCommBank Health Insights Report 2025
Commonwealth Bank of Australia · May 2025 · 7 million transaction records (Apr 2024–Mar 2025)
Hard transaction data confirming 18–24 year olds increased health spending by only 3.1% — below Health CPI of 4.1%, a real-terms decline. They specifically cut physiotherapy, dental, and optometry. Spending data, not survey — one of the strongest evidence sources for the problem.

Official ABS data confirming 9.6% of Australians delayed or did not see a GP due to cost. 26.0% waited longer than acceptable for a GP appointment. Age-disaggregated data confirms younger Australians face disproportionate access barriers.
ABS — Patient Experiences 2024–25

Royal Australian College of GPs · September 2025
Average gap fee for a standard appointment increased to $39. 8.8% of Australians delayed care due to costs in 2023–24. Strong GP-sector evidence for the bulk-billing crisis directly relevant to CostClear's design rationale.
