01Problem Statement

Young Australians aged 18–29 are cutting back on healthcare in real terms — not because they're healthy, but because the system is opaque. Most don't know what bulk-billing means, what Medicare covers them for, or what care will cost before they show up. When something feels wrong, 37% go to Google first. The information to help them exists. The entitlements are already funded. The problem is that no one has made the system legible for the generation navigating it alone for the first time.

02Who · What · Where · Why · How
👤WHO is Jordan?
Jordan is a 23-year-old casual hospitality worker living in Footscray, Melbourne. They moved out of home at 19 and onto their own Medicare card. They earn an irregular income — sometimes full weeks, sometimes nothing — which makes financial planning, including healthcare, genuinely hard.

Jordan didn't grow up with parents who explained the health system. They've never had a regular GP and wouldn't know how to find one that's affordable. They're not disengaged from health as a concept — they care — but the system feels like it was built for someone else.
🤔WHAT does Jordan experience?
Jordan experiences the health system as opaque and anxiety-inducing. Every interaction involves uncertainty about cost. They've shown up to a GP before and been surprised by a $70 out-of-pocket gap they didn't expect. That happened once — they haven't gone back since.

They know they should be getting skin checks (they grew up in Queensland), blood tests, and probably a dental check. But "should do" and "will do" are separated by a wall of: How much will it cost? Where do I even go? Do I need a referral? Is this worth it?
📍WHERE does Jordan seek help?
First stop: Google. A symptom, a question, a vague worry — it goes into a search bar. Jordan doesn't distinguish between "reliable health information" and "the first result." They read Reddit threads, symptom checker websites, and health forum posts.

Second stop: texting a friend. "Is this normal?" is a common 11pm message.

Third stop (only if urgent): hospital ED. Jordan's been once with a bad infection. It was a 4-hour wait. They're determined not to repeat it — but if something feels serious, they don't know where else to go.
💡WHY doesn't Jordan engage?
Cost uncertainty is the primary barrier. It's not that Jordan can't afford $0 — it's that they don't know if it will be $0 or $95 until they're already at the counter. That asymmetry of information is enough to avoid it entirely.

Navigation complexity is the secondary barrier. The Medicare website, MyGov, and even Healthdirect feel like they were written for people who already understand the system. Terms like "bulk-billing," "gap fee," "Medicare rebate," "item number" — these are not intuitive.

Absence of urgency is the third. Nothing has gone seriously wrong yet. The "someday" list keeps growing.
📱HOW does Jordan want to be helped?
Plain language, not jargon Jordan wants to understand what something means in one sentence, not after reading a help article. "This is free for you" beats "eligible for Medicare bulk-billing rebate under item 715."
Cost shown upfront, always The number one thing that would change Jordan's behaviour: knowing what something costs before committing. Not a range, not "it depends" — a specific, honest estimate.
Mobile-first, no account needed Jordan won't create an account for a healthcare app. They'll use it on their phone during a 10-minute break at work. The entire value has to be delivered in under 60 seconds.

01Core Features
🧾
EntitleCheck — Your Medicare entitlements, personalised
Enter age, sex, postcode → get a plain-English list of what Medicare covers you for right now
Core Feature
Problem it solves
Most 18–29 year olds have a Medicare card but no idea what it actually covers. They've never been told about age-specific screenings, 45-year health assessments (though not relevant yet — but chronic disease plans are), or preventive checks. This turns invisible entitlements into a readable, personal checklist.
User flow
1
Enter age, sex, postcode — no login, no account
2
App generates personalised Medicare coverage list from MBS item numbers
3
Each item shows: what it is · what it costs · when you're due · plain-English explanation
4
One tap → nearest bulk-billing provider for that exact service type
Datasets used
MBS Item Schedule (age/sex rules) National Preventive Health Strategy AIHW Screening Guidelines ABS Postcode→SA2 Concordance
What makes it unique
No existing consumer app surfaces Medicare entitlements in personalised, plain English. The framing — "here's what the system owes you" — has never been done at this level of accessibility for young Australians.
Solves Jordan's pain point
"Has no idea what their Medicare card actually entitles them to beyond going to the doctor."
💸
CostClear — Know what you'll pay before you go
Shows estimated out-of-pocket cost per service type by postcode, before you book
Core Feature
Problem it solves
Cost uncertainty is the #1 reason 18–29s avoid booking. The gap between "Medicare covers it" and "what I'll actually pay at the counter" is enormous and invisible until it's too late. CostClear makes that gap visible before any commitment.
User flow
1
Search by service type: GP, skin check, blood test, dental, physio
2
See providers near you colour-coded: 🟢 Free · 🟡 Low-cost · 🔴 Gap fee expected
3
Tap any provider → see exact estimated gap + what Medicare rebates back
4
Link directly to booking page or phone number
Datasets used
MBS Bulk-Billing Stats (quarterly) Healthdirect NHSD API Health.gov.au GP Provider Data ABS SEIFA 2021 (disadvantage index) ABS Postcode→SA2 Concordance
What makes it unique
HotDoc and Healthdirect show availability. Neither shows cost. This is the gap. SEIFA layer ensures high-disadvantage postcodes surface free options first — automatically.
Solves Jordan's pain point
"Avoids booking because cost is unknown until they're already at the counter — and that happened before."
🚦
RightDoor — Where should I actually go?
Non-diagnostic care pathway comparison: GP vs urgent care vs telehealth vs pharmacist — cost and wait time side-by-side
Core Feature
Problem it solves
37% of Australians go to Google first for health concerns. 43% end up in ED when they didn't need to. The problem isn't that people don't care — it's that the decision of "where to go" is genuinely confusing. RightDoor replaces the Google search with a structured, non-diagnostic decision aid.
User flow
1
Select concern category (not symptom diagnosis — broad category only, no clinical input)
2
Answer 2–3 urgency questions (no clinical assessment, no risk stratification)
3
App shows GP · Telehealth · Urgent Care · Pharmacist — side by side
4
Each shows: estimated cost · nearest option · estimated wait time
Datasets used
MyHospitals ED Wait Times API MBS Telehealth-Eligible Items Health.gov.au After-Hours GP Data Medicare Urgent Care Clinics (137 national) Healthdirect NHSD API ABS Postcode→SA2 Concordance
What makes it unique
No tool currently shows all four care pathways side-by-side with cost and proximity simultaneously. This is the Google replacement — not a symptom checker, not a forum, not advice.
Solves Jordan's pain point
"Googles health concerns at 11pm and ends up more anxious than before."
📅
WellTrack — Preventive health schedule
A personal timeline of free screenings you're due for, with reminders and direct booking links
Key Feature
Problem it solves
Young Australians know they should get a skin check "at some point." There's no trigger, no deadline, and no consequence until it's serious. WellTrack turns vague intention into a personal schedule grounded in Medicare's own clinical guidelines.
User flow
1
Set a profile: age, sex, postcode — stored locally, no account required
2
App generates a timeline: overdue · due now · upcoming (12 months)
3
Tap any item → what it is, why it matters, what it costs, what's covered
4
Add to calendar or book directly through CostClear layer
Datasets used
MBS Item Schedule (age/sex) AIHW National Health Survey National Preventive Health Strategy
What makes it unique
Bridges EntitleCheck (what you're covered for) and CostClear (how to access it) by adding the time dimension. The behaviour gap isn't lack of information — it's the absence of a personal deadline.
Solves Jordan's pain point
"Has a mental 'someday' list of health things to do — with no trigger to ever act on it."

03Why Each Component Must Exist Together
The critical insight: None of these features work in isolation. EntitleCheck without CostClear creates awareness without action. CostClear without EntitleCheck helps people who already know what to search for. RightDoor without WellTrack helps in moments of crisis but doesn't build habits. WellTrack without CostClear creates to-do lists with no confidence to act on them. Together, they form a complete legibility layer over the health system.
❌ Without the full system
Jordan uses Healthdirect. Finds a GP nearby. No cost info. Books. Gets surprised at the counter. Doesn't go back for 2 years. Returns to Google the next time something feels wrong.
✅ With MediClear's full system
Jordan checks EntitleCheck. Sees they're overdue for 3 things. CostClear shows a free clinic 1.4km away. Books all 3 in one session. WellTrack reminds them next year. The loop closes — and keeps closing.
